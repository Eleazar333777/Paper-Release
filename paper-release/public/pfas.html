The History of PFAS

From industrial innovation to environmental crisis - explore the timeline of how "forever chemicals" became one of the most pressing water contamination challenges of our time.

1930s

The Discovery

Dr. Roy Plunkett accidentally discovers PTFE (polytetrafluoroethylene) while working for DuPont. This discovery marks the beginning of the PFAS era, though the full implications wouldn't be understood for decades.

1940s

Commercial Production Begins

DuPont begins commercial production of Teflon. The U.S. military recognizes the potential of these materials for various applications, leading to increased research and development during World War II.

1950s

Consumer Market Expansion

PFAS chemicals enter the consumer market through non-stick cookware, stain-resistant fabrics, and food packaging. Companies like 3M begin producing PFOS and PFOA for various industrial applications.

1960s-70s

Industrial Applications Expand

PFAS use expands dramatically across industries including aerospace, electronics, and firefighting. Aqueous film-forming foam (AFFF) containing PFAS becomes standard for fighting petroleum fires at military bases and airports.

1980s

First Health Concerns

Internal company studies begin to reveal potential health risks associated with PFAS exposure. DuPont scientists find PFOA in the blood of their own employees and note potential links to health problems.

1990s

Environmental Persistence Discovered

Scientists begin to understand that PFAS don't break down naturally in the environment or human body. The term "forever chemicals" emerges as researchers realize these substances bioaccumulate and persist indefinitely.

2000s

Major Legal Battles Begin

High-profile lawsuits emerge, including cases in West Virginia and Ohio where communities sue DuPont for PFOA contamination. The EPA begins investigating PFAS contamination and health effects more seriously.

2009-2015

Industry Phase-Out Begins

Major manufacturers begin voluntary phase-out of PFOS and PFOA production in the United States. However, they're often replaced with other PFAS compounds that may pose similar risks but have less research data.

2016-2020

Widespread Contamination Revealed

EPA monitoring reveals PFAS contamination in drinking water systems across the United States. Studies show PFAS are present in the blood of 99% of Americans, highlighting the scope of exposure.

2020-Present

Regulatory Response & Innovation

The EPA establishes drinking water standards for PFAS. States implement their own regulations. Meanwhile, researchers and companies like CETARA develop advanced treatment technologies to remove PFAS from water systems.

Future

The Path Forward

Continued research into PFAS remediation technologies, stricter regulations, and development of safer alternatives. Projects like CETARA's membrane filtration research offer hope for effective, scalable solutions to PFAS contamination.
